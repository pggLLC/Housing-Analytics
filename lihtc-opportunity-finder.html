How is the opportunity score calculated?

Each QCT or DDA gets a 0–100 score from four components:

  • Recency (35%) — years since the most recent LIHTC project was placed in service in the area. Capped at 25+ years. Areas with no funded LIHTC ever get the top score. Signals saturation headroom — CHFA's geographic-distribution scoring rewards gap areas.
  • Housing need (30%) — Scorecard v2 composite (PR #885) for the containing county: a percentile-normalised blend of tenure-blended cost burden, deep-need share, affordability pressure (home price ÷ HHI), and HUD worst-case-needs signal. Higher = more acute need.
  • Basis-boost (20%) — QCT: 40 of 100, DDA: 40, both: 60, neither: 0. IRC §42(d)(5)(B) basis boost is a single election, but areas in BOTH a QCT and a DDA often have a stronger competitive case in QAP scoring.
  • Population feasibility (15%) — <500: 0 pts, 500–2,000: 50, ≥2,000: 100. LIHTC projects need a leasing pool. Acts as a floor that filters out small rural tracts where a 50-unit project couldn't absorb.

Sources: HUD LIHTC database (716 CO projects, YR_PIS 1987–2020), HUD QCT designations (224 CO tracts), HUD DDA designations (10 CO areas — county-based non-metro + ZIP-based metro), HUD CHAS for housing-need composite, ACS for population.

Caveats: Projects with YR_PIS = 8888 in HUD's source data (placeholder for pipeline projects) are excluded from the last-funded computation. Point-in-polygon assignment uses project lat/lng vs. QCT/DDA polygon — projects right on a boundary edge may be ambiguous.
